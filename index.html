Your Body As The Instrument and Paintbrush:
An Embodied HCI Creative Project

Julia Jünger Aalborg University, Copenhagen, Denmark Medialogy MSc Embodied Interaction Mini Project
Section I

Project description

This project investigates how embodied interaction can transform the human body into the primary interface for interraction, becoming both a musical instrument and a visual medium. Grounded in theories of embodied metaphors and movement analysis, the system enables users to generate and manipulate audiovisual output through physical movement. This project takes foundation in Paul Dourish’s conception of embodied interaction as meaning-making through situated action, alongside Laban Movement Analysis as a lens for understanding movement quality. Implemented in TouchDesigner, the system creates a real-time feedback loop between motion, sound, and visuals, encouraging users to experience interaction as a continuous, felt process.

Section III

Introduction

Embodied interaction describes how people interact with computational systems through bodily actions situated in the physical world. According to Paul Dourish, embodied interaction is the creation, manipulation, and sharing of meaning through engaged interaction with artifacts1. Instead of abstract interfaces such as keyboards or menus, interaction emerges through physical movement and spatial engagement. In interactive art systems, this approach allows users to influence audiovisual outcomes through gestures and bodily motion, turning the body itself into an interface. In this project, body movement controls both visual particles and musical parameters, creating an embodied feedback loop between movement, sound, and visual form. The user's body becomes an interface for controlling sound and visuals. Through different mappings, meaning emerges through movement-based interaction, not buttons or menus. The artwork is co-created through embodied action. The body thus functions as the primary interface, replacing conventional input devices. Movement in physical space directly influences both visual and sonic outputs, allowing the user to interact with the system through gesture and spatial motion. By combining visual particle behavior with real-time audio processing, the system encourages users to become more aware of their bodies and movements within space. Through these mappings, the user’s body acts not only as an input device but also as a creative instrument. Movements can simultaneously shape the visual artwork and modulate the music, turning bodily motion into a musical performance and a digital painting.

Section IV

Problem Statement

When developing interactive systems in symbiosis with the body, many parameters have to be considered. Not only is it vital to have an understanding of the affordances of a body in interaction with digital systems, but also the cognitive foundations of visual, spatial, and auditorial mappings. Different visual and artistic choices will--not always dependent on the developers intention--prompt different movement and induce particular ways of moving. Despite advances in interactive systems, many digital interfaces still rely on discrete, symbolic inputs that fragment the relationship between bodily action and expressive outcome. This creates a gap between what the body does and what the system produces, limiting the depth of engagement, especially in creative contexts. The central challenge, therefore, is how to successfully align design choices with the intended affect on the user (which in this scope is making the user aware of their body and movement). The problem is to design an interaction paradigm where movement qualities (not just positions) shape outcomes, users experience interaction from a first-person, felt perspective, and sound and visuals respond in ways that reinforce embodied understanding.

Section V

Theoretical Foundations & Related Work

Drawing on Paul Dourish, this project addresses the absence of “meaningful engagement” in many interfaces1. At the same time, without frameworks like Laban Movement Analysis, systems risk ignoring the expressive richness of movement itself. The primary theoretical foundation comes from Paul Dourish, whose work establishes that interaction is fundamentally embodied, social, and situated. His argument rejects the notion of interaction as abstract input/output, instead positioning it as a process through which meaning is enacted in real time. This project directly adopts this stance: the system does not translate movement into commands; it allows meaning to emerge through continuous engagement. The second framework used for development is the Laban Movement Analysis (LMA). LMA is central to the conceptual and design approach. It breaks movement into key components of effort, space, body, and shape, allowing for a terminology to explain different types of movement within space. Ludwig Wittgenstein famously said that "The limits of my language means the limits of my world"1. Excluding LMA, we lose a language for describing otherwise abstract or not easily separatable concepts, and with that, perhaps the ability to perceive and differentiate movement at all. LMA in this project helped guide the design choices to include a wide range of movement, rather than mapping simple spatial dimensions.

Section VI

Development & Design

The audiovisual system was implemented in TouchDesigner using a particle-based system consisting of approximately 58,000 particles. Movement detection was implemented using optical flow analysis through a custom MoveDetector component. The detected motion vectors were connected to the “wind” parameter of the particle system, allowing user movement to influence particle direction and produce natural-looking flow dynamics. Particle color is controlled by total movement by making the system register a difference between alpha levels in the current screen and a slightly delayed screen. This allows for creation of thresholds, where the lower range of total alpha difference means constricted, slow movement (Mapping most closely to the Laban parameters of Indirect, Light, Sustained, Bound); and a high alpha difference results from large, rapid movement (Free, Sudden, Strong, Direct).

The audiovisual feedback is controlled in three ways. Horizontal movement (left/right) controls stereo panning, positioning the sound within the stereo field. Vertical movement (top/bottom) controls the cutoff frequency of a low-pass filter applied to the music. This mapping introduces a spatial, directional dimension to the experience beyond mere movement qualities. The audio interaction was implemented by spatially segmenting the camera image. By cropping the camera feed into horizontal and vertical regions, the system was able to separately analyze left/right and top/bottom movement. Horizontal movement was connected to stereo panning, allowing the user’s position in space to shift the sound between the left and right speakers. Vertical movement controlled a low-pass filter applied to the music, allowing users to modulate the brightness of the sound by moving up or down within the camera frame. This mapping creates continuous changes in the music based on the user’s spatial movement, reinforcing the embodied relationship between body motion, sound, and visual feedback. Thus: - Fast, sharp movements (time/weight effort) produce more intense visual and sonic changes - Smooth, sustained motion (flow) results in continuous, fluid modulation - Directional movement (space) directly influences spatial audio and particle trajectories Rather than explicitly encoding Laban categories, the system implicitly responds to them through motion capture and mapping, allowing expressive qualities to emerge naturally. To increase interactivity and stylistic variety, three different modes were created. Each mode changes the particle color scheme, particle speed, and background music. Users can switch between modes using hand movements detected by the camera, inspired by early camera-based interaction systems such as the PlayStation EyeToy, successfully making the body part of the interface. Mode switching was implemented by analyzing changes in RGB values in the camera feed’s top left corner. These changes were converted into numerical values and evaluated using logic operators that activate when the RGB change exceeds a specified threshold. A timer was added to prevent accidental switching and ensure that mode changes occur only through intentional gestures. The timer output was scaled to a range of 0–360 and used to control the radius of a circle, visually representing the progress of the mode change. The "Dream" mode represents the slowest mode, with calm music and slow particle speed. "Hype" has rapid, quickly evaporating particles and high-energy music. "Flow" lies in between. Beta user testing of the different modes showed that the change in particle speed and music prompted different movement styles–"Hype" generated Slashes, Dabs, and Flicks, while "Dreamy" made users explore Glides and Floats. Together, these mappings carefully and intentionally allow people to use their bodies not just as a UI element, but also an instrument and a paintbrush.

Section IX

Discussion

The system needs no introduction or instructions; meaning is created real-time through bodily interaction. The project therefore successfully aligns with Dourish's theories of meaning through interacgiona nd the body as an interface. Future development would include correcting the movement encoding to not be dependent on the size of your body on the screen. The alpha difference creates a functional threshold of the user's distance to the screen. Whereas it is not a methodological restriction, as many similar body interactive designs are also dependent on the distance positioning, it could be interesting to explore a version where the particles and music react similarly independent of your distance to the screen.
